From logic to probability

Propositional logic

Propositional logic is a language used to represent knowledge and reason about the truth or falsity of propositions.

An intelligent agent uses propositional logic to draw conclusions from a set of known facts (propositions). For example, given the propositions \(P \rightarrow Q\) (If it is raining, then the street is wet) and \(P\) (It is raining), the agent can infer \(Q\) (The street is wet) using Modus Ponens. This logical reasoning allows the agent to make decisions, predict outcomes, and understand the relationships between different pieces of knowledge.

Propositional logic provides a foundation for more complex forms of logic, such as first-order logic, which incorporates quantifiers and predicates, enabling more detailed and nuanced representations of knowledge.

Propositional logic also provides a foundation for probability theory, where logical propositions are assigned probabilities, allowing for reasoning under uncertainty/with incomplete information (“plausible/inductive reasoning”). This integration of logic and probability is essential for developing robust models in artificial intelligence and machine learning.

Probability theory extends propositional logic to allow reasoning about the uncertainty of propositions.

Propositional variables (aka atomic propositions)

These are basic statements that can either be true or false. For example:

  • \(P\): It is raining
  • \(Q\): The street is wet

Logical connectives (from atomic to compound propositions)

  • AND (\(\land\)): Conjunction, true if both operands are true
  • OR (\(\lor\)): Disjunction, true if at least one operand is true
  • NOT (\(\lnot\)): Negation, true if the operand is false
  • IMPLIES (\(\implies\)): Implication, true if the antecedent is false or the consequent is true
  • IF AND ONLY IF (\(\iff\)): Biconditional, true if both operands are either true or false

Truth tables

A tool used to determine the truth value of complex propositions based on the truth values of their atomic components.

Inference rules for valid arguments

Rules that allow the derivation of new propositions from existing ones. Common rules include Modus Ponens and Modus Tollens.

Propositional logic

Propositional logic is a language used to represent knowledge and to reason about the truth or falsity of propositions based on their logical relationships within that knowledge.

A proposition is a statement that is either true or false. For example:

  • \(P\): It is raining
  • \(Q\): The street is wet

\(P\) and \(Q\) are atomic propositions.

They can be combined to form compound propositions.

Compound propositions are formed by combining atomic propositions using logical connectives.

The logical connectives are:

  • \(\land\): and
  • \(\lor\): or
  • \(\neg\): not
  • \(\implies\): implies (if-then)
  • \(\iff\): if and only if

Logical connectives are defined by truth tables.

\[ \begin{array}{|c|c|c|c|c|c|c|c|} \hline P & Q & P \land Q & P \lor Q & \neg P & P \implies Q & \neg P \lor Q & P \iff Q \\ \hline T & T & T & T & F & T & T & T \\ T & F & F & T & F & F & F & F \\ F & T & F & T & T & T & T & F \\ F & F & F & F & T & T & T & T \\ \hline \end{array} \]

\(P \implies Q\) and \(\lnot P \lor Q\) have the same truth table, i.e., they are logically equivalent:

\(P \implies Q \equiv \lnot P \lor Q\)

Logical implication (\(P \implies Q\)) simply indicates that if \(P\) is true, then \(Q\) must also be true. It does not imply any causal relationship between \(P\) and \(Q\).

For example, if:

  • \(P\): 2 + 2 = 4
  • \(Q\): All cats are mammals

Then:

\(P \implies Q\): If 2 + 2 = 4, then all cats are mammals

is a true statement! It is also true in a world where 2 + 2 = 4 is false.

For example, if:

  • \(P\): A person has the APOE44 genotype
  • \(Q\): A person has Alzheimer’s disease

Then:

\(P \implies Q\): If a person has the APOE44 genotype, then he/she has Alzheimer’s disease

is a true statement! It is also true in a world where a person does not have the APOE44 genotype.

Urn with two balls

You draw one ball out.

What’s the probability that you draw the white ball?

\(W\): You draw the white ball.

\(B\): You draw the blue ball.

\(P_1 = W \lor B\)

\(P_2 = \lnot (W \land B)\)

\(E = P_1 \land P_2\)

\(H = W\)

Urn with N balls

Background knowledge:

  • Urn with N balls, M of which are white and N-M blue.

  • You draw one ball out.

  • What’s the probability that you draw a white ball?

\(P(H \mid E) = \frac{M}{N}\)

Probability distributions

Binomial Probability

Given:

  • \(E_B\): Binomial evidence

  • \(K\): Number of successes

  • \(N\): Number of trials

  • The probability of \(K\) successes given the binomial evidence and number of trials can be written as:

\[ P(K = k \mid E_B, N = n) = \binom{n}{k} p^k (1 - p)^{n - k} \]

where:

  • \(\binom{n}{k}\) is the binomial coefficient, which represents the number of ways to choose \(k\) successes out of \(n\) trials.

  • \(p\) is the probability of success on a single trial.

Here is the R code to compute this probability:

# Number of successes
k <- 3

# Number of trials
n <- 10

# Probability of success on a single trial
p <- 0.5

# Compute the binomial coefficient
binom_coeff <- choose(n, k)

# Compute the probability
probability <- binom_coeff * p^k * (1 - p)^(n - k)

# Print the result
probability
[1] 0.1172

Explanation

  • ( ): This is the binomial coefficient calculated as choose(n, k) in R.
  • ( p ): Probability of success on a single trial. You can adjust this value based on your specific problem.
  • ( (1 - p) ): Probability of failure on a single trial.
  • The formula: Multiplies the binomial coefficient by the probability of getting ( k ) successes and the probability of getting ( (n - k) ) failures.

By executing the R code in an RMarkdown document, you will get the probability of observing exactly ( k ) successes in ( n ) trials given the specified probability of success.
